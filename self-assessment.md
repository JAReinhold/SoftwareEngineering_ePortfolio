# Professional Self-Assessment

*How I Learned to Think Like a Software Developer*

---

Introduction
If you asked me a few years ago what a software developer did, I probably would have said, "They write code." Technically that's true, but after completing this degree, I think that's one of the smallest parts of the job.
I see now that software development is really about solving problems. It's about looking at something complicated, breaking it into manageable pieces, recognizing patterns, and accepting that there is almost never a single "correct" solution. Every design decision is a tradeoff. Every feature introduces complexity. Every shortcut becomes tomorrow's technical debt. Learning to recognize those tradeoffs has probably been the biggest lesson of this entire program.
One thing I discovered about myself throughout this degree is that I genuinely enjoy improving things. Whether it's an old Java project, an Android application, a database schema, or even something completely unrelated to software, I naturally find myself asking, "How could this be better?" Looking back, that mindset was always there. The Computer Science program simply gave me the tools to turn curiosity into practical problem solving.
That realization became especially clear during this capstone. Revisiting projects I completed earlier in the program, years ago at some point, was almost like looking through a time capsule. The projects worked, and at the time I was proud of them. But now I’ve noticed things I never would have recognized before. Repeated logic. Weak validation. Missed opportunities to simplify code. Data structures that worked but weren't the best choice. None of those were failures they just showed my lack of experience and are great contrast to how I’ve grown.
Becoming More Than Someone Who Writes Code
Early in the program my goal was straightforward: make the assignment work, get the grades, and learn a little something along the way. If the application compiled, passed the requirements, and produced the expected output, I considered it successful.
Somewhere along the way that changed, and now I think about the next developer who might read my code. I think about whether someone could understand my design decisions six months from now. I think about whether validation belongs in one location instead of being repeated throughout the application. I think about performance, maintainability, security, scalability, and how today's design decisions affect tomorrow's work.
Ironically, I don't think this happened because I learned another programming language, or how to properly write loops, it happened because I learned how experienced developers think. That shift in perspective changed everything. I stopped asking, "Does it work?" and I started asking if my solutions were the best way I knew how to solve the problem at hand. Even though there usually isn't a perfect answer, learning how to evaluate those tradeoffs has become one of the skills I'm most proud of developing.
One thing I didn't expect to improve throughout this degree was my ability to explain technical concepts. Writing software is only part of being a developer. Explaining design decisions, documenting code, and communicating with stakeholders are just as important. Throughout this capstone I found myself spending almost as much time explaining why I made certain decisions as I did making them. Writing enhancement narratives and recording my code review reinforced that good communication makes good software even better.
Earlier in the program, security wasn't something I thought much about beyond passwords and authentication. Courses like Secure Coding changed that perspective completely. I began looking at software differently, asking questions about input validation, error handling, data protection, and how an application could fail if someone intentionally misused it. That mindset carried into my capstone enhancements, where stronger validation and thoughtful design became just as important as adding new functionality.
Lessons Beyond the Classroom
One thing that made this journey unique was completing it while serving active duty in the United States Navy. Nearly ten years in the military taught me lessons that translated surprisingly well into software development. Attention to detail matters more than just about anything. Documentation matters. Accountability matters. Sometimes you're handed a problem you've never seen before, and nobody expects you to know the answer immediately, though they do expect you to figure it out regardless. That mindset carried over into every programming course I completed.
There were plenty of assignments where I had no idea how I was going to solve the problem when I first read it, especially coming in with little to no actual Software Engineering experience. Earlier in my education that uncertainty would have been intimidating. By the end of this program, it became almost expected. I learned to trust the process, research the problem, break it into smaller pieces, experiment, make mistakes, learn from them, and keep moving forward. Looking back, I think that confidence may be more valuable than any specific language or framework I learned.
The Story Told by My Portfolio
The artifacts included in this portfolio weren't chosen because they're the most complicated projects I've completed. I chose them because they clearly demonstrate how my thinking has evolved.
The software engineering enhancement with my ContactService application challenged me to improve organization, readability, validation, and maintainability rather than simply adding features, which is what I was initially planning. The algorithms and data structures enhancement with my Android app MisManaged reminded me that writing software isn't just about making something function correctly, Choosing better data structures and simplifying logic can make software easier to maintain while still improving efficiency at the same time. Then using MisManaged again, the database enhancement reinforced that software doesn't exist in isolation. Good applications rely on well-designed data just as much as well-written code. Improving the application's database design strengthened both its functionality and its reliability.
Looking Forward
If this degree taught me anything, it's that becoming a software developer isn't a finish line, it's the beginning of a career built around continuous learning. Technology changes too quickly for anyone to ever know everything. What matters is developing the ability to adapt, ask better questions, and remain curious enough to keep learning. I think that's the skill this program strengthened more than any other.
As I prepare to graduate, I don't feel like someone who has finally learned everything there is to know. I feel like someone who now has the confidence and foundation to continue learning for the rest of my career after the Navy. Whether I retire from the Navy or transition into civilian software development sooner, I hope to bring that same mindset with me: stay curious, solve problems thoughtfully, write software that I'm proud to stand behind, and never stop looking for ways to improve.
Looking back and reflecting on the years of my life learning in this degree program, all things considered, I think the biggest lesson I’ve learned is that anyone can learn how to write code, the difference is in those that learn how to think.
